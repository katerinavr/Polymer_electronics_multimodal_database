Tunable green electrochromic polymers via direct arylation polymerization†

Liyan You,‡a   Jiazhi He‡a  and  Jianguo Mei *a  
Author affiliations

* Corresponding authors

a Department of Chemistry, Purdue University, 560 Oval Drive, West Lafayette, IN 47907, USA
E-mail: jgmei@purdue.edu

Abstract

A method to tune the hues of neutral green conjugated electrochromic polymers (NG-ECP) via direct C–H arylation polymerization (DArP) was developed. We used 4,7-dibromo-2,1,3-benzothidiazole and 2,7-dibromofluorene to randomly copolymerize with ProDOT via DArP and successfully obtained a series of green polymers. On varying the ratio of 4,7-dibromo-2,1,3-benzothidiazole and 2,7-dibromofluorene, we can readily adjust the relative intensity of two absorption bands in the visible-light wavelength region, which leads to four green polymers with different hues being obtained. Specifically, increasing the ratio of 4,7-dibromo-2,1,3-benzothidiazole leads to the enhancement of the polymer absorption intensity in the long wavelength (∼550–700 nm) region, while increasing the ratio of 2,7-dibromofluorene will lead to the increase of the polymer absorption intensity in the short wavelength (∼400–500 nm) region. Via the random direct arylation polymerization of multi-component monomers in various ratios, we can precisely control the absorption spectra of neutral green conjugated electrochromic polymers (NG-ECPs) in the visible-light wavelength region. The electrochemical and optical properties, spectroelectrochemical behaviors, and electrochromic performance of these NG-ECPs were characterized and they showed great potential for electrochromic devices such as smart windows and transmissive displays.

Introduction

In the last few decades, electrochromic conjugated polymers (ECPs) have attracted a great deal of attention for their processability, flexibility, and tunable colors through structural design.1 The potential application of ECPs in displays,2,3 smart windows,4,5 E-paper,6 and data-storage devices7 drives the scientific community to create all kinds of conjugated polymers for electrochromic devices. However, most of them are either red or blue in their neutral states, and the green color electrochromic polymers have been rarely reported. In 2004, Wudl et al. reported the first neutral green conjugated ECP (NG-ECP)8 and there have been only a few other updates until now.9–22 Great challenges still remain in designing new monomers to tune the spectra of ECPs to achieve green conjugated polymers in the neutral state, since NG-ECPs have dual-band absorption in both short wavelength (∼400 nm–500 nm) and long wavelength (∼550 nm–700 nm) regions of visible light. Moreover, the color of the as-polymerized NG-ECP was not tunable in most of the cases.16,23 For example, Reynolds et al. reported a donor–acceptor NG-ECP of dioxythiophene-benzothiadiazole.14,16 The color of the NG-ECP can only be tuned by changing the structures of the polymer repeat-unit. However, preparation of the monomers was complicated and needed multiple steps. Therefore, a generalized and viable method is desired to realize such unique dual-band absorption of ECPs, so that the molecular spectra can be tuned specifically to be transmissive in a certain range of wavelength.

Herein, we reported our approach for achieving tunable NG-ECPs via direct C–H arylation polymerization (DArP). DArP is an environment-friendly polymerization method for donor–acceptor (D–A) type conjugated polymers.24–28 The easy-to-make monomers, simple purification procedures, and low metal residue have made it an ideal method for the synthesis of conjugated ECPs. It is already known that the homopolymer of 3,4-propylenedioxythiophene (ProDOT) had magenta color and narrow absorption around 550 nm.29 By copolymerizing ProDot with 1,3-benzothiadiazole as an acceptor, a blue conjugated ECP which had a main absorption in the red-light area can be synthesized.30 Moreover, a yellow color conjugated ECP which had a main absorption in the blue-light area can be achieved by using fluorene as the acceptor and ProDOT as the donor.31–33 Thus, if an approach to combine the absorption character of blue and yellow ECPs can be discovered, we would be able to obtain green polymers which can absorb in both regions. Herein, we used 4,7-dibromo-2,1,3-benzothidiazole and 2,7-dibromofluorene to randomly copolymerize with ProDOT via DArP and successfully obtained a series of NG-ECPs. With the increasing ratio of electron-deficient 2,1,3-benzothidiazole, the intensity of the first absorption peak (∼600 nm) decreased. At the same time, with the addition of fluorene, the intensity of the second absorption peak (∼450 nm) increased due to the increase of the ProDOT–fluorene twist angle. With the addition of both 2,1,3-benzothidiazole and fluorene, we can readily adjust the relative intensity of the two absorption bands to obtain four green polymers with different hues.

Results and discussion

Polymer and oligomer synthesis

The synthesis of monomers is shown in the ESI.† The synthesis of polymers is shown in Scheme 1. At first, we made P1 to test our assumption that inserting 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole and fluorene into poly-3,4-propylenedioxythiophene (PProDOT) will lead to two-band absorption in the red-light and blue-light regions. We used 0.25 equivalents of 4,7-dibromo-2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (compound 6), 0.35 equivalents of 2,7-dibromo-9,9-dihexyl-9H-fluorene (compound 3), and 0.4 equivalents of dibromo-ProDOT (compound 5) to randomly copolymerize with 1.0 equivalent of ProDOT (compound 4) via DArP and obtained polymer P1. The solution state UV-Vis spectrum of P1 shows two peaks with a higher absorption peak at 440 nm and another peak at 594 nm (Fig. 1a). The valley point is located at 529 nm. Although the color of the polymer was not green, the two-band absorption spectrum encouraged us to further optimize the monomer ratio. We increased the equivalents of compound 6 from 0.25 to 0.3 and decreased the equivalents of compound 3 from 0.35 to 0.3. Then, we obtained polymer P2 via the DArP method. Interestingly, the UV-Vis spectrum of P2 shows that the intensity of these two peaks was reversed compared with P1. The higher absorption peak changes to ∼594 nm and the weaker peak locates at ∼440 nm (Fig. 1a). These results indicated that we could control the absorption intensity in the red-light and blue-light regions by varying the equivalents of compound 6 and compound 3. Based on these results, the green conjugated polymers can be obtained by either increasing the ratio of compound 6 in P1 or increasing the ratio of compound 3 in P2. Therefore, we added 0.3 equivalents of compound 6, 0.35 equivalents of compound 3, and 0.35 equivalents of compound 5 to randomly copolymerize with 1.0 equivalent of compound 4 to prepare a new polymer P3. P3 had a dark green color and a standard two-band UV-Vis absorption in the solution state (Fig. 1b). Following the same strategy, we successfully made another three green polymers (P4, P5, and P6) by changing the ratio of compound 6 and compound 3. We calculated the x/y/z composition of each polymer according to the value of 1H NMR integration and the results are shown in ESI Table S1.† All the number-average molecular weights (Mn) of the polymers were determined using GPC and the data are shown in Table 1.

Optical and electrochemical properties

The UV-Vis absorption spectra of four green polymers were examined both in CHCl3 solution (Fig. 1b) and in the thin-film state (Fig. 2a). Two distinct peaks were found both in the solution state and thin-film state. Comparing the UV-vis spectra in the thin-film state with the solution state, obvious red-shifts can be observed for both peaks due to the efficient self-stacking of the backbone in the solid state. From P3 to P6, a blue shift in the solution and film state was observed for both peaks, with the increase of the ratio of compound 6 and compound 3. Moreover, the valleys of the two peaks shift from 520 nm to 483 nm in the solution state and shift from 539 nm to 491 nm in the film state (Fig. 1b and Fig. 2a).

Cyclic voltammetry (CV) was used to investigate the electrochemical behaviour of the green polymers (Fig. 2b). The polymer thin-films were deposited onto Pt disk electrodes via drop-casting from CHCl3 solution at room temperature. Pt and Ag/AgCl wires were used as the counter and reference electrodes, respectively. The polymer films were cycled at a scan rate of 40 mV s−1 between −0.2 V and 1.3 V until the cyclic voltammograms became stable and reproducible. The polymer films were found to be stable during the p-type doping within this potential range. According to the onset potentials of oxidation of green polymers and their optical band gaps, the HOMO energy levels varied from −4.97 eV to −5.27 eV; the LUMO energy levels changed from −3.33 eV to −3.66 eV. Upon careful examination of the data presented in Table 1 and Fig. 2b, we found that oxidation potentials increased when increasing the ratio of compound 6 and compound 3.

Spectroelectrochemical analysis

The spectroelectrochemical analyses of P3, P4, P5 and P6 were carried out with in situ UV-vis-NIR while changing the applied potential (Fig. 3). The thin films of the green polymers were deposited (ca. 40 mg mL−1) onto indium tin oxide (ITO)-coated glass slides via spin-coating from CHCl3 solution at room temperature. The polymer films were cycled within the corresponding potential range based on the CV studies (Fig. 2b) at a scan rate of 40 mV s−1 in 0.2 M TBAP/PC electrolyte solution until they exhibited stable and reproducible switching. Upon stepwise oxidation, the intensity of the two absorption bands in the blue-light and red-light regions began to diminish, and the absorption in the near-IR region started to increase because of the formation of polarons and bipolarons. It was worth noting that the absorption valley of all the green polymers didn't change too much during the oxidation process, indicating that the thin films remained green all the time. The residue absorption in the visible region made the polymer films exhibit a transmissive light green in the bleached state.

Electrochromic properties

Electrochromic switching studies of P4 and P6 were performed to study the transmittance changes when applying square-wave potential steps with switching intervals ranging from 20 to 3 s. The chronoamperometry analysis is shown in Fig. S11.† Both green polymers had a similar optical contrast in the two absorption bands. At 20 s intervals, for the shorter wavelength absorption band, the optical contrasts of P4 and P6 were 51% and 56%. For the longer wavelength absorption band, the contrasts were 32% and 35% respectively. For both green polymers, optical contrasts at the shorter wavelength (420 nm/430 nm) decreased significantly at 3 s switching intervals, while the optical contrast at the longer wavelength (600 nm) only decreased slightly.

The colorimetric performances of four green polymers were tested with spin-coating films on ITO-coated glasses. The color changes resulting from the redox switching of the films were characterized based on the ‘Commission Internationale de l′Eclairage’ 1976 L*a*b* color standards. The L*a*b* color spaces of all polymers were obtained by using the Star-Tek colorimetry software with a D50 illuminant and 2-degree observer.31 Fig. S12† shows the lightness change of the green polymer films in their colored state and bleached state. With increasing voltage, all the films showed an increase of the L* value. P3 started to change the L* value at ∼0.5 V vs. Ag/AgCl, while the L* value of P6 began to increase at ∼0.8 V vs. Ag/AgCl, which is consistent with the oxidation onsets shown in the CVs of the thin films of the green polymers (Fig. 2b). To mathematically analyze the colors of all the green polymers, their a* and b* in both colored and bleached states are shown in Fig. 4. The more negative the value of a* is, the greener the polymer appears. The positive and negative value of b* indicates a yellow and blue hue, respectively. In the colored state, all of the four polymers had a positive value of b* and a negative value of a*, which means they all present a chartreuse (yellow-green) color. In previous literature, most of the green conjugated ECPs showed a very different color in the colored state and bleached state, and it's rare to see a green polymer that shows a similar type of color in the colored state and bleached state.20 Our polymers provided more choice for the green ECP family.

The electrochemical stability of the green polymers was tested with spin-coating thin films on ITO-coated glasses. Fig. S13† reveals that the polymer thin films exhibited a decrease of about 20% of transmittance at 430 nm after 644 cycles under ambient conditions. An electrochromic device was developed with P4 as the working electrode and a PEDOT/PSS layer as the counter electrode. The device architecture is shown in Fig. 5a. A PEDOT/PSS layer with a thickness of 0.05 mm controlled by a square tape (1.6 × 1.6 cm) in the center was coated on the outside margin of the ITO substrate by drop-casting. The tape was removed after the PEDOT/PSS layer was dried. By using this architecture, the deep blue color of the PEDOT/PSS would not affect the green polymer layer which was coated in the center of another ITO substrate.

Here, the PEDOT/PSS layer showed no color change during the switching of the device indicating that the micrometer thick PEDOT/PSS layer had a much higher redox capacity than the green polymer layer. Thus, this is also an ‘unbalanced configuration’ investigated by Hassab et al.34 When a potential of 1.2 V was applied to the device, it became transmissive but when the potential was switched to −0.5 V, the device recovered to its green state (Fig. 5b). The optical contrast of the device is 42.3%, and the switching time for the device to achieve 95% of the contrast is 25.9 s and 5.9 s for bleached and colored processes, respectively (Fig. 5c).

Conclusions

A series of conjugated polymer electrochromes composed of 3,4-propylenedioxythiophene (ProDOT) units, 9,9-dihexyl-9H-fluorene units and the electron-deficient core 2,1,3-benzothiadiazole (BTD) was rationally designed to achieve neutral green colors. We highlight the random direct arylation polymerization of multi-component monomers to precisely control the absorption spectra of polymers in the visible light region. The electrochemical and optical properties, spectroelectrochemical analysis, and electrochromic performance of these green polymers were tested. This straight-forward strategy that can adjust the absorption spectra of ECPs is applicable to other polymer systems with different types of co-monomers.
